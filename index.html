Optional Add-On

Take a Cooking Class in Florence

A hands-on Tuscan cooking class is a Florence highlight. Many start with a Mercato Centrale market tour, then you cook and sit down to eat fresh pasta, sauces, and seasonal dishes, often paired with Chianti. Both schools below book directly and can arrange a private class for your group. Best slotted into a free Florence afternoon or evening: Day 2 or Day 7 work well, and a market-tour version pairs naturally with your Day 5 Mercato Centrale visit.

Mama Florence
mamaflorence.com

Professional chef and sommelier-led, hands-on classes near Porta Romana, in a kitchen with a wine cellar and outdoor patio. Pasta, gelato, wine-paired menus, and private group experiences with a market-tour option.

Group classes ~€89–139/pp · private for the group
In Tavola
intavola.org

Long-running school in the Oltrarno (Via dei Velluti), a short walk across the river. Small, hands-on classes in pasta and Tuscan classics, with market-tour versions available.

Hands-on classes ~€75–120/pp
💡 For all eight of you, ask each school about a private group class, often better value and more relaxed than joining a public class. Book a few weeks ahead, the popular evening slots fill up.
As Seen on Searching for Italy

Stanley Tucci's Florence

Stanley Tucci filmed his love letter to Tuscan food in and around Florence. These are the places and dishes he sought out, leaning into the region's humble "poor food" classics done exceptionally well.

Osteria del Cinghiale Bianco
cinghialebianco.net

A Tucci favourite in a 14th-century tower on Borgo San Jacopo in the Oltrarno, a short walk across the river. He praised the panzanella here. Also known for truffle flan, pappardelle with wild boar ragù, and roast suckling pig.

Oltrarno · book ahead
Dalla Lola

In working-class Santo Spirito, chef Matilde Pettini celebrates Florence's humble, historic dishes. Tucci's pick for traditional Tuscan cooking done with real care.

Santo Spirito · reserve direct
Nugolo

Where Tucci tried a contemporary, foamy reinvention of the classic Tuscan panzanella. The modern, creative side of Florentine cooking.

Contemporary Tuscan
Mercato di Sant'Ambrogio

The liveliest market in Florence, where Tucci shopped for the meat to make bistecca alla Fiorentina. A great morning wander with food stalls and a lunch counter.

Market · mornings
🥩 Tucci's Florence must-eat is the bistecca alla Fiorentina, the towering Tuscan T-bone served very rare. It is sold by weight (al etto) and meant to be shared, so order one for the table.

Get It Done

Before You Go

The most important things are passports and flights. Everything else can wait a little. Colour-coded tags show what needs to happen when.

Pack Light

Packing Essentials

The golden rule: pack lighter than you think you need to. You will walk on cobblestones, navigate airports, and move between two cities. Less is genuinely more.

✈️ Flying internationally

Check in online 24 hours before your flight and download your boarding pass. Arrive early, international departures need more time for passport control and security, so allow at least 3 hours. Set your watch to Italian time as soon as you board, and drink water, not alcohol, on the flight.

Dress the Part

What to Wear & What Not To

Italians notice what you wear, not in a judgmental way, just in the way that style is part of daily life. Nothing here requires shopping, just a few things to keep in mind when you pack.

✓ Do Wear

  • Clean leather or canvas shoesWhite leather sneakers (Veja, New Balance 550s) are perfectly Italian. Dirty running shoes are not.
  • Well-fitted trousers or dark jeansSlim or straight cut. Avoid anything baggy or distressed.
  • Linen or cotton dresses and blousesFloaty and breathable, perfect for September heat.
  • Simple, solid-colour topsQuality basics in muted tones: navy, white, terracotta, olive.
  • A light scarf or wrapEssential for churches, and a layer for cool evenings.
  • SunglassesItalians love them. Any style, the more expressive the better.
  • A structured tote or small leather bagCrossbody bags work perfectly for day trips.

✗ Avoid

  • Baseball capsThe single biggest giveaway. Leave them at home.
  • Cargo shortsEspecially with multiple pockets. Italians simply do not wear these.
  • Graphic tees with logos or slogansCollege logos, sports teams, "I ♥ Italy" shirts: instant tourist flags.
  • A giant hiking backpack in the cityAn expedition-size bag screams tourist and is a pickpocket target.
  • Flip flops in the cityFine at the pool. Not for cobblestone streets or restaurants.
  • Walking with your map app openStudy the route first. Looking lost is fine, advertising it isn't.

⛪ The church rule, strictly enforced

To enter the Vatican, Sistine Chapel, or any Italian church, shoulders and knees must be covered, for everyone including the kids. Guards will turn you away at the door, no exceptions. This applies to all eight of you. September is warm, so a lightweight scarf or sarong in your bag solves everything.

Parla Italiano

Useful Italian Phrases

🔑 The one phrase that unlocks Italy

Start any interaction with "Buongiorno!" and end it with "Grazie mille!" (a thousand thanks). Walking into a shop, a museum, a winery, always greet first. Italians find it rude to launch straight into a request. That one habit alone will make your whole trip smoother. And remember: a "caffè" is always an espresso, drunk standing at the bar for the cheaper price.
